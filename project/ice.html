Causes and Effects of Melting

Climate change has caused global temperatures to slowly rise. These rising temperatures have led to the melting of ice in Earth's polar regions.

Ice reflects the sun's light back into space. So, as more ice melts, even more heat will stay on Earth, contributing further to climate change.

In addition, as more ice melts, the sea level will rise, changing Earth's coastlines.

NASA, or the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, uses satellites such as GRACE to track the melting of ice sheets in Earth's polar regions. NASA's satellites use lasers to measure ice sheet mass. This mass has been in decline over the past few decades due to ice melting.

Ice Sheet Information

Greenland Ice Sheet

The Greenland ice sheet is a large ice sheet that makes up 79% of the surface of Greenland. The ice sheet is massive, at over 1.7 million square kilometers, and 2-3 kilometers thick. This makes the Greenland ice sheet the second largest body of ice on Earth. If this sheet melted completely, sea levels would rise by about 7.2 meters.

Antarctic Ice Sheet

The Antarctic ice sheet is the largest ice sheet on Earth, taking up more than 14 million square kilometers, and being approximately 2 kilometers thick. If this ice sheet melted completely, sea levels would rise by 60 meters.

Other Ice Sheet Information

As ice sheets melt, they release fresh water into the ocean, changing the ocean's salinity levels. This could have devastating effects on corals and other marine organisms, who depend on saltwater to survive.

Air Pollution: Biggest DIRECT Threat on Human Health

  • Respiratory Infection
  • Heart Disease
  • Lung Cancer
  • Heat Island Impacts: The increased temperatures in the day and night as well as the increased air-pollution levels lead to heat-related deaths and illnesses. For example, respiratory difficulties, heat cramps, heat exhaustion, and heat stroke. Dramatic temperature increases can result in higher rates of mortality than average.

Higher Risk of Natural Disaster

Heat Waves

With the continuous release of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, the coldest and warmest daily temperatures will increase by at least 5 degrees F within the next century. There is expected to be 20-30 more days over 90 degrees F in most areas within the century, according to the National Climate Assessment. Within the last decade, daily record high temperatures have happened about twice as often as record lows - a stark increase from the 1950’s where the record highs and lows were about equal.

Storms

Tropical storms are greatly affected by climate change because their main causes have to do with sea surface temperatures and sea level. Warmer ocean temperatures and the rising of sea levels is causing tropical storms to intensify greatly and to increase their negative impacts. This means that hurricanes are more likely to cause significantly worse damage and higher death rates if no action is taken.

Fires

Wildfires depend on a number of factors including soil moisture, temperature, and the amount of vegetation in an area - factors that are all affected by climate change. The number of wildfires within the western United States has doubled between 1984 and 2015. An average annual increase of 1 degree C would cause the median burned area per year to increase by as much as 600 percent in certain regions.

Floods

The rising temperature of the atmosphere increases the atmosphere’s ability to hold, and in turn, distribute more water. Within the Northeast US alone, extreme storms generate about 27 percent more moisture than they did 100 years ago. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration concluded that current rains are at least 40 percent more likely to occur and 10 percent more intense due to factors affecting climate change.
